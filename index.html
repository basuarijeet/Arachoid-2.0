Latest Happenings

Web 3 Utility Project (EventBear)

We, at EventBear are pioneering in the WEB3 space with utility NFT solutions to tackle real world problems pertaining to Event Booking and Ticketing operations. NFTs are unique digital tokens stored on a blockchain. Once they are created on the blockchain, they can be sold to customers digitally. Customers then store NFTs in a secure wallet that they can access on any device. In the ticketing system, we can mint the required number of NFT tickets on their choice of a blockchain platform(Solana). We can code the NFTs to set a sale price or run the sale as an auction where people can bid for tickets.A buyer purchases NFT-based tickets directly from the ticketing company(we). On receiving payment, a smart contract triggers and a ticketing database sends an NFT ticket to the buyer. This technology also enables the NFT holders to sell or trade the same NFT on secondary markets with royalties and gas fees applying for the same. It also enables the customers to hold onto the rare NFT tickets as souvenirs for reminiscing the past.
Pioneers: Arijeet Basu, Hemanth Kumar, Ross Gerritsen.

Aurora (Borealis, Australis)

Aurora - named after Aurora, the Roman goddess of dawn, and Boreas, the Greek god of the north wind - or 'Northern Lights,' is a natural phenomenon that occurs close to the North Pole. It's when charged particles from the Sun collide with gas atoms in Earth's atmosphere and then the planet's magnetic field makes green, red, purple streaks of light dance across the sky.

Beaches of Maldives

It's almost impossible not to talk about the Maldives in cliché: bright-white super-soft sand and pristine waters in a thousand hues of blue. It's all true – and so is the fact that the Maldives is home to a disproportionate number of the world's best beaches.

The Milky Way

The Milky Way is a huge collection of stars, dust and gas. It’s called a spiral galaxy because if you could view it from the top or bottom, it would look like a spinning pinwheel. The Sun is located on one of the spiral arms, about 25,000 light-years away from the center of the galaxy. Even if you could travel at the speed of light (300,000 kilometers, or 186,000 miles, per second), it would take you about 25,000 years to reach the middle of the Milky Way.
